Every trail running gear list starts with shoes. Fair enough — shoes matter. But you can have perfect shoes and still have a terrible run if the rest of your kit is wrong.

Here are the seven things that actually affect your trail experience, ranked by how much they'll ruin your day if you get them wrong. This post contains affiliate links. I may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you.

## 1. Hydration Vest

This is the single most important piece of gear after shoes. A bad vest bounces, chafes, and makes you dread anything over 10km.

The Salomon ADV Skin 12 is the benchmark for a reason. It fits like a second skin, the soft flasks are easier to drink from than a bladder hose, and the pockets are actually accessible while running. Not cheap (~$150), but you'll use it on every single run.

Budget pick: the Nathan VaporAir. Slightly bouncier, but half the price.

## 2. Headlamp

If you run early mornings or evenings — and you will — a headlamp isn't optional. The Petzl Actik Core is the sweet spot: rechargeable, 450 lumens, and light enough to forget it's there.

Avoid anything over 100g. Heavy headlamps bounce and give you a headache on descents.

## 3. Gaiters

Trail gaiters keep rocks, dirt, and debris out of your shoes. They look slightly ridiculous. They're also the difference between stopping every 2km to empty your shoes and just running.

Dirty Girl gaiters are the standard. They attach to your shoe's laces and wrap around your ankle. Lightweight, cheap, and they come in patterns loud enough to spot from space.

## 4. Nutrition

Bonking on a trail is worse than bonking on a road. There's no corner shop at kilometre 15. For anything over 90 minutes, carry fuel.

Gels work. Spring Energy gels taste better than most and sit easier on the stomach. But honestly, a few rice balls or salted potatoes in a ziplock work just as well and cost almost nothing.

## 5. Rain Shell

A packable rain shell weighs 100-200g and fits in your vest pocket. You won't need it 90% of the time. The other 10% it'll save your run — or your life if conditions turn.

The Salomon Bonatti is the trail running standard. Packs to the size of a fist.

## 6. Trekking Poles (Yes, Really)

For ultras and long climbs, poles save your legs. Collapsible carbon poles (like Black Diamond Distance Z) fold down small enough to strap to your vest.

You don't need them for every run. But on anything with 1,000m+ of climbing, they make the ascents 20% easier and save your quads for the descents.

## 7. First Aid — The Bare Minimum

Carry a small kit: blister plasters, a compression bandage, antihistamine, and a whistle. That's it. You're not performing surgery — you're covering the basics until you get back to the trailhead.

## Hot Take: Ditch the GPS Watch for Short Runs

Your phone with a free app like Strava does 90% of what a $500 GPS watch does. If you're running under 2 hours on marked trails, you don't need wrist-based navigation. Save the money for a better vest or actual race entries.

For ultras and backcountry navigation? Yeah, get the watch. A Coros Apex 2 is the best value in the category right now.
