Small kitchen advice usually falls into two categories: "Renovate your kitchen" (helpful if you have a spare ten grand) or "Get rid of everything" (helpful if you don't cook).

If you actually use your kitchen — like, daily — you need real storage solutions that work within the space you've got. No knocking out walls. No Marie Kondo-ing your only frying pan.

## Vertical Space Is Free Space

The biggest mistake in small kitchens is ignoring the walls. You have metres of unused vertical space between your countertop and ceiling.

### Wall-Mounted Magnetic Knife Strip

Knife blocks eat counter space. A magnetic strip on the wall holds your knives, frees up a chunk of counter, and actually makes them easier to grab. Mount it high enough that kids can't reach it.

### Hanging Rail System

A simple rail with S-hooks along the backsplash holds utensils, mugs, small pans — anything with a handle or loop. IKEA does a stainless steel version that holds surprising weight.

### Over-Cabinet Door Organisers

The inside of your cabinet doors is prime unused space. Stick-on or over-door organisers can hold spice jars, foil rolls, cleaning supplies, or chopping board racks.

## The Counter Rule

In a small kitchen, every item on the counter needs to earn its spot. If you don't use it daily, it goes in a cabinet.

**Stays on the counter:**
- Kettle
- Toaster (if you use it daily)
- Knife strip (on the wall)
- Dish soap

**Goes in a cabinet:**
- Stand mixer (unless you bake 3+ times a week)
- Air fryer
- Coffee machine you use twice a week
- Bread bin with one stale loaf

This is not minimalism for its own sake. Counter space in a small kitchen is your workspace. Every appliance sitting out is workspace you've lost.

## Cabinet Organisation

### Shelf Risers

The space above stacked plates is wasted. A wire shelf riser creates a second level inside the cabinet, so you can store mugs above plates without stacking them dangerously high.

### Lazy Susans in Corner Cabinets

Corner cabinets are the Bermuda Triangle of kitchens. Things go in and never come out. A lazy Susan (turntable) makes the entire space accessible — spin it and everything comes to you.

### Pull-Out Baskets

If you have deep base cabinets, the stuff at the back is functionally invisible. Retrofit pull-out wire baskets that slide like drawers. They're not cheap, but they effectively double your usable cabinet space.

## Fridge Organisation

A disorganised fridge wastes food, which wastes money. In a small kitchen, you can't afford to buy replacements for things you already own but couldn't find.

**Top shelf:** Ready-to-eat items, leftovers, drinks.
**Middle shelf:** Dairy, eggs, deli items.
**Bottom shelf:** Raw meat, fish (contained, so nothing drips).
**Drawers:** Fruits and vegetables, separated.
**Door:** Condiments, butter. Not milk — the door is the warmest part of the fridge.

Clear stackable containers are worth the investment here. They let you see everything at a glance and stop the "mystery tupperware at the back" problem.

## Pantry Alternatives

No pantry? No problem. Here are alternatives:

- **Over-door rack on a coat cupboard** — If you have a hall cupboard near the kitchen, the inside of the door can hold a spice rack or dry goods shelf
- **Rolling cart** — A narrow rolling cart (15-20cm wide) fits between your fridge and counter. Pull it out when you need it, push it back when you don't
- **Wall-mounted shelves** — Open shelving above head height for items you use weekly but not daily

## The One In, One Out Rule

In a small kitchen, storage is a zero-sum game. Every new item needs a home, and if there's no home, something else has to leave.

Before buying a new gadget, ask:
1. Where will it live?
2. What will I remove to make space?
3. Will I use it more than once a month?

If you can't answer all three, you don't need it — no matter what the Amazon reviews say.

## What Actually Works

After two years of optimising a 7 square metre kitchen, here's what made the biggest difference:

1. **Magnetic knife strip** — freed up an entire section of counter
2. **Shelf risers in every cabinet** — doubled visible storage overnight
3. **Clear containers in the fridge** — cut food waste noticeably
4. **The counter rule** — keeping only daily-use items out made cooking less stressful
5. **Rolling cart** — the single best purchase for a small kitchen

None of these require tools, drilling, or landlord permission (except the knife strip, which needs two screws).
